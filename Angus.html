Security and Privacy of COVID-19 track and trace systems

Different systems have been employed by various continents and countries. A few Asian countries have used phones to track the public who they think may have COVID-19 with no consent from the user. Whereas European governments have used anonymised IDs for users to track their movements, whilst the US are employing the same technology that an advertising company would use to track large gatherings of people (Abbas et al., 2020). This shows that depending on the laws, political standing, and consent of the people, there are multiple different applications with varying degrees of security that can be produced. This report will look at different models individually and all models as a whole to look in-depth at the security issues and potential solutions to some vulnerabilities.

Due to the track and trace system requiring data of users and the other users around them. The English government put in place a system that kept the individual’s data safe through a random identification number instead of holding their name and other sensitive data. This data could not be seen by the government or the NHS for privacy and security reasons (O’Dowd, 2020). Through a random identification number (ID) this stops the availability for hackers to see the name of each person and where they live as the ID is completely disassociated with an individual’s details. This system creates trust with the general public as security is an important consideration for users, especially when such sensitive data about themselves could be potentially lost.

There is however a concern over the ability to still see the phone type of a user in a specific area through Bluetooth. This technology could assist in the process of malicious reconnaissance that someone may carry out to identify a person (Magklaras, 2020). Bluetooth therefore has an inherit flaw where due to its design developers can check what devices are Bluetooth powered. This causes a security concern as potential hackers could abuse this power for reconnaissance purposes. As the public already fear government tracking, knowing that the COVID-19 app could assist in such a process could prevent potential downloads of the app resulting in less effective contact-tracing.

Furthermore, the security and potential risks of contact tracing apps creates trust issues with the general public. A focus group study conducted by the journal “Health Expectations” concluded that a there was a major group of people with worries about their data’s security and privacy. Where the public were particularly concerned with the governments use of their data and believed that the app could be used as further spying for the government (Williams et al., 2021). The findings of this study show that an increase in public relations work is required to alleviate the misconceptions the public may have about app security.

Apple and Google teamed up to use their technology to ensure secure and accurate contact tracing, as two reputable companies this could install a feeling of security in the public. Through their partnership they created an API (application programming interface) that would store only necessary data whilst continuing to prioritise people’s security over their private data (Burgess, 2020). By using trusted company’s such as Apple and Google this will instil more trust with the public and lead to an increase in downloads of the contact tracing applications. Thus, keeping possibly infected people in self-isolation and preventing the spread of Covid-19.

The NHS X app has admitted it works with various big tech company’s such as Google, Microsoft, Amazon Web Services and many more. This extensive use of industry outside of the governments public sector can create concern as the government need to rely not on themselves but on private tech-companies to follow the correct GDPR guidelines (Guinchard, 2020). As the contact tracing application is only effective providing there is multiple users on the NHS X app, the less trust the users have with various private-sector companies, the less people will download the track and trace application.

As many of the worldwide contact-tracing apps require GPS permission this could cause concern for the user. Especially when the mobile network provider may not even ask for the users permission for their location to be tracked (Borra, 2020). Borra goes further in detail where she explains that these apps could potentially open opportunities for the government to increase their reconnaissance or even private-sector corporations using the data for advertisement purposes. As privacy is a basic human right, users should at minimum be given the option to be tracked with their own consent which many applications do not allow.

The next study looked deep into the code that built 41 different contact-tracing applications to investigate the different private data that was collected for contact-tracing. The investigators found that there are privacy issues within a collection of the contact-tracing apps which they believe could be fixed. One of these possible exploits is that user ID’s are static whereas a system with greater security would include a system of ID’s that change regularly (Wen et al., 2020). Apps that don’t take the required time to update their security could be jeopardising their user’s data that should be as secure as possible especially when dealing with sensitive information such as location and distance from other users.

The IEEE have advised applications should use a blockchain security approach to remove the security issues of a central database. This is due to the risks that come with a centralized system where applications like Google and Apples contact-tracing app, including many others could risk a “trajectory attack” where a user’s profile could be rebuilt from server data (Xu et al., 2020). It Is worrying that the end user’s data could again be targeted from a security issue. Even IEEE researchers could find a flaw yet two major private-sector company’s such as Google, and Apple fail to create a blockchain security approach.

As the first track and trace systems to be employed were Australia’s CovidSafe and Singapore’s TraceTogether there were key lessons to be learned from such systems before various other countries developed theirs. The main problem with both apps is that they assume the end user is a healthy, English speaking individual whereas there are plenty of immigrants, children and disabled users that either cannot speak English or will struggle to operate the application (Abbas and Michael, 2020). The total disregard of those who may need assistance is why the initial apps failed to succeed. Whereas later applications learned from such mistakes and adapted their technology to suit multiple languages and provide accessibility.
