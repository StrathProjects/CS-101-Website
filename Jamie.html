The Use of Technology in Combatting the Social/Mental Health Impact of Covid-19

The outbreak of covid-19 forced people around the world to change the way they work, receive an education, and interact with one another. A big change to everyone’s lives due to the pandemic was the government-imposed lockdowns, as well as outside of lockdowns people having to self-isolate in their homes at any point if they are in contact with someone infected with the virus. People are informed if they’ve been in contact with anyone who has covid-19 by using track and trace, meaning it is always important to test yourself for covid if you show any symptoms so that you know to self-isolate and so that people you’ve been in contact with are told as soon as possible, which will help stop the spread. But according to polling performed by the UK government, “just 17% of people showing symptoms are subjecting themselves to tests” (Stokel-Walker, 2021), which means that very few possibly infected people are finding out if they are infected; mainly due to being afraid of how a positive test result would impact them and their family. The main reasons people fear a positive test result or being told they’ve been in contact with an infected person, is that they would struggle to earn income while isolating or that they could infect their family. If you test positive you must self-isolate immediately, which results, in some cases, in not being able to work, and for some people it is difficult to self-isolate due to restrictive space in their home and concerns over multiple people having to share spaces like a bathroom or kitchen with the infected person (Matukas, Dhalla and Laupacis, 2020).

The need to share rooms in the home makes self-isolating from others in your household or accommodation nearly impossible which increases people’s fears of infecting those around them. The other big fear from self-isolating, of losing income, is a big one, as not all people were eligible for furlough pay if they couldn’t work from home, which could add to people’s stress and cause them to use their savings or take out loans to pay for everyday necessities. This could also pile onto people’s pre-existing debt or money struggles.If you test positive, or you’ve been in contact with an infected person, there is a massive fear for your health as well as the wellbeing of your family and others around you. This has caused a large increase in mental health decline across the entire population and isn’t helped by “repeated media images of severely ill people, dead bodies and coffins” (Tsamakis et al., 2021, p.2) which has caused more fears especially since people wouldn’t be able to say goodbye to sick or dying loved ones. Increased levels of anxiety, panic and depression have all stemmed from these fears, not to mention increase in loneliness due to not being able to see friends or family while you’re isolating.

During giant outbreaks like with covid-19, quite often the number of people who suffer mentally far outweighs the number of people who were infected by the virus. This means that during crises like the pandemic there should be a large focus on mental health help as well as help with virus treatment, but when the outbreak first began, covid-19 had interrupted or stopped “critical mental health services in 93% of countries world¬wide while the demand for mental health is increasing” (Tsamakis et al., 2021, p.4). The mental health issues required a solution quickly before it got out of control, thankfully as mental health was in decline countries were adapting more to the virus, meaning they managed to come up with solutions to the mental health problem. Many countries soon began to offer over the phone mental health help and consultation where face to face sessions were no longer possible. A large portion of people who had mental health issues before the pandemic did start to use this service, and even people who only began having mental health struggles during the pandemic started to go and get help, with “around one in five young people and one in six adults” (Mind charity, 2021, p.20) getting mental health help for the first time, over the course of the pandemic, so it isn’t all negative.

Sadly, given these new methods of receiving help and support for any mental health issues, “more than one in five (21%) adults and one in seven (15%) young people” (Mind charity, 2021, p.20) didn’t seek help with their problems because they didn’t think it was worth taking up consultation time. This over the phone method was a great help for people suffering with mental health problems but people also used other things to try and improve their mental wellbeing. People have been using many different forms to cope with lockdowns and isolation. Many have used types of technology such as social media and zoom to stay connected with friends and family, which has given people more contact with the loved ones they can’t see face to face. Unfortunately, even with this online connectivity many people think it hasn’t really improved their mental state, possibly due to the amount of covid-19 related information on the internet and social media. Because of the nature of online information people can scroll endlessly through different videos and articles on covid-19, which leads to a lot of confusion with the changing covid-19 info as well as more fear over the virus and what regulations to follow (Garfin, 2020).

Covid-19 has proved to be quite a challenge, but people have managed to change and adapt how they do things and over quite a short period. People have begun using track and trace and doing tests to check if they are infected and to notify others who they’ve been in contact with if they are. Some have swapped to working online, getting healthcare support over the phone, and contacting family over zoom and social media if they’re isolating. The mental health of the public has experienced a decline due to the covid-19 outbreak and issues it created, but the healthcare system has worked hard to combat this in a covid-19 safe way.
